Phase 1:

Goal is to go from raw price data to clean, informative feature matrix that our regime models can learn from.

> Regime models don't just look at raw prices... 
    > They look at statistical properties of returns that tend to be stable within a regime and shift when a regime changes.

> Step 1:
    > Download raw price data using yfinance

> Step 2:
    > Log returns
        > Raw prices are non-stationary (they trend upward forever), which breaks most statistical models.
            > Log returns r_t = ln(P_t / P_{t-1}) are approximately stationary, time-additive and close to normally distributed.
                > These are exactly the assumptions that HHMs and MS models assume.

> Step 3:
    > Feature Engineering
        > Model needs features that actually change between regimes:
            > rolling_vol - standard deviation of returns over a 21-day window
            > rolling_return - mean return over a 21-day window
            > rolling_skew - negative skew tends to cluster in crisis regimes
            > vix_level - if we include the VIX (vol index) data, it is generally accepted as a direct fear guage
            > yield_spread - (10Y-2Y) - inverted yield curves historically precede recessions
    
> Step 4:
    > Cleaning
        > Rolling windows introduce NaN data for the first 21 rows, so we need to drop such rows.
            > Also might be worth 'winsorising' extreme outliers (replace the instances with datapoints falling within 5-95 percentile)

> Step 5:
    > Standardisation
        > HMMs estimate Gaussian distributions, so scale matters...
            > StandardScaler is a scikit-learn tool which standardises features by removing the mean and scaling it to unit variance.
                > StandardScaler should be fit only on the training set to avoid look-ahead bias.

Step 6:
    > Train/test split
        > Must be chronological
            > pre 2020 training and post 2020 is out of sample test
